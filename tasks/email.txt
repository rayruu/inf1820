			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Email address</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr __reader_view_article_wrap_02831984580492941__"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Decltype_mail.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Example of an email address.</div>

<p>An <b>email address</b> identifies an email box to which <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> messages are delivered. A <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Internet_email_address" title="Non-Internet email address">wide variety of formats</a> were used in early email systems, but only a single format is used today, following the standards developed for <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> mail systems since the 1980s. This article uses the term <i>email address</i> to refer to the <b>addr-spec</b> defined in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5322">RFC 5322</a>, not to the <b>address</b> that is commonly used; the difference is that an address may contain a display name, a comment, or both.</p>
<p>An email address such as <i>John.Smith@example.com</i> is made up of a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#Local-part">local-part</a>, an @ symbol, then a case-insensitive <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#Domain_part">domain</a>. Although the standard specifies the local part to be case-sensitive, in practice the mail system at <i>example.com</i> may treat <i>John.Smith</i> as equivalent to <i>JohnSmith</i> or even as <i>johnsmith</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and mail systems often limit their users' choice of name to a subset of the technically valid characters. In some cases they also limit which addresses it is possible to send mail to.</p>
<p>With the introduction of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">internationalized domain names</a>, efforts are progressing to permit non-ASCII characters in email addresses. Companies like Microsoft, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://productforums.google.com/forum/#!topic/apps/8vxYh8-fMqg">Gmail</a> has announced compatibility with EAI and companies like Data Xgen Technologies has in fact started offering <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.chopchop.news/blog/internet-globalization-utilize-email-for-non-english-idns">EAI compatible email</a> server.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The transmission of electronic mail within the Internet uses the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol" title="Simple Mail Transfer Protocol">Simple Mail Transfer Protocol</a> (SMTP), defined in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet standard">Internet standards</a> <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5321">RFC 5321</a> and <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5322">RFC 5322</a>, and extensions like <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6531">RFC 6531</a>. The mailboxes may be accessed and managed by users with the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_Office_Protocol" title="Post Office Protocol">Post Office Protocol</a> (POP) or the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Message_Access_Protocol" title="Internet Message Access Protocol">Internet Message Access Protocol</a> (IMAP) with email client software that runs on a personal computer, mobile device, or with <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webmail" title="Webmail">webmail</a> systems that render the messages on a screen or on paper printouts.</p>
<p>The general format of an email address is <i>local-part@domain</i>, and a specific example is <i>jsmith@example.com</i>. An address consists of two parts. The part before the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/@" class="mw-redirect" title="@">@</a> symbol (local-part) identifies the name of a mailbox. This is often the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Username" class="mw-redirect" title="Username">username</a> of the recipient, e.g., <i>jsmith</i>. The part after the @ symbol (domain) is a domain name that represents the administrative realm for the mail box, e.g., a company's domain name, <i>example.com</i>.</p>
<p>When <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol#Mail_processing_model" title="Simple Mail Transfer Protocol">delivering email</a>, a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mail_server" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail server">mail server</a> uses the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain name system">domain name system</a> (DNS) to look up the mail exchanger record (MX record) for the recipient's domain (the part of the email address on the right of @). The returned MX record contains the name of the recipient's mailserver. The MTA next connects to this server as an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMTP" class="mw-redirect" title="SMTP">SMTP</a> client.</p>
<p>The local part of an email address has no significance for intermediate mail relay systems other than the final mailbox host. Email senders and intermediate relay systems must not assume it to be case-insensitive, since the final mailbox host may or may not treat it as such. A single mailbox may receive mail for multiple email addresses, if configured by the administrator. Conversely, a single email address may be the alias to a distribution list to many mailboxes. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_alias" title="Email alias">Email aliases</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_mailing_list" title="Electronic mailing list">electronic mailing lists</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#Sub-addressing">sub-addressing</a>, and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catch-all_(Mail)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catch-all (Mail)">catch-all</a> addresses, the latter being mailboxes that receive messages regardless of the local part, are common patterns for achieving a variety of delivery goals.</p>
<p>The addresses found in the header fields of an email message are not directly used by mail exchangers to deliver the message. An email message also contains a message envelope that contains the information for mail routing. While envelope and header addresses may be equal, forged email addresses are often seen in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_spam" title="Email spam">spam</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a>, and many other Internet-based scams. This has led to several initiatives which aim to make such forgeries easier to spot.</p>
<p>To indicate the message recipient, an email address also may have an associated display name for the recipient, which is followed by the address specification surrounded by angled brackets, for example: <i>John&nbsp;Smith&nbsp;&lt;john.smith@example.org&gt;</i>.</p>
<p>Earlier forms of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Internet_email_address" title="Non-Internet email address">email addresses on other networks than the Internet</a> included other notations, such as that required by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X.400" title="X.400">X.400</a>, and the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UUCP" title="UUCP">UUCP</a> <i>bang path</i> notation, in which the address was given in the form of a sequence of computers through which the message should be relayed. This was widely used for several years, but was superseded by the Internet standards promulgated by the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a> (IETF).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Syntax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The format of email addresses is <code>local-part@domain</code> where the local part may be up to 64 characters long and the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain</a> may have a maximum of 255 characters<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>—but the maximum of 256-character length of a forward or reverse path restricts the entire email address to be no more than 254 characters long.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The formal definitions are in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5322">RFC 5322</a> (sections 3.2.3 and 3.4.1) and <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5321">RFC 5321</a>—with a more readable form given in the informational <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3696">RFC 3696</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.rfc-editor.org/errata_search.php?rfc=3696">associated errata</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local-part">Local-part</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Local-part">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The local-part of the email address may use any of these <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> characters:</p>
<ul>
<li>uppercase and lowercase <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Latin_(Unicode_block)" title="Basic Latin (Unicode block)">Latin</a> letters <code>A</code> to <code>Z</code> and <code>a</code> to <code>z</code>;</li>
<li>digits <code>0</code> to <code>9</code>;</li>
<li>special characters <code>!#$%&amp;'*+-/=?^_`{|}~</code>;</li>
<li>dot <code>.</code>, provided that it is not the first or last character unless quoted, and provided also that it does not appear consecutively unless quoted (e.g. <code>John..Doe@example.com</code> is not allowed but <code>"John..Doe"@example.com</code> is allowed);</li>
<li>space and <code>"(),:;&lt;&gt;@[\]</code> characters are allowed with restrictions (they are only allowed inside a quoted string, as described in the paragraph below, and in addition, a backslash or double-quote must be preceded by a backslash);</li>
<li>comments are allowed with parentheses at either end of the local-part; e.g. <code>john.smith(comment)@example.com</code> and <code>(comment)john.smith@example.com</code> are both equivalent to <code>john.smith@example.com</code>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition to the above ASCII characters, international characters above U+007F, encoded as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a>, are permitted by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6531">RFC 6531</a>, though mail systems may restrict which characters to use when assigning local-parts.</p>
<p>A quoted string may exist as a dot separated entity within the local-part, or it may exist when the outermost quotes are the outermost characters of the local-part (e.g., <code>abc."defghi".xyz@example.com</code> or <code>"abcdefghixyz"@example.com</code> are allowed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This does not necessarily follow from the BNF grammar and seems to be a misreading of RFC 3696 as it described unquoted text (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Conversely, <code>abc"defghi"xyz@example.com</code> is not; neither is <code>abc\"def\"ghi@example.com</code>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This does not necessarily follow from the BNF grammar and seems to be a misreading of RFC 3696 as it described unquoted text. In fact, RFC 3696 says that abc\@ghi@example.com is valid, and a backslash escaped quoted-pair is allowed anywhere a quoted-string is allowed. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Quoted strings and characters however, are not commonly used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="While this intuitively seems true, I&#39;d like to see a reference. In any event, it seems irrelevant when describing the syntax. The sequence xyz123 is also not commonly used, but that&#39;s immaterial. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5321">RFC 5321</a> also warns that "a host that expects to receive mail SHOULD avoid defining mailboxes where the Local-part requires (or uses) the Quoted-string form".</p>
<p>The local-part <code>postmaster</code> is treated specially—it is case-insensitive, and should be forwarded to the domain email administrator. Technically all other local-parts are case-sensitive, therefore <code>jsmith@example.com</code> and <code>JSmith@example.com</code> specify different mailboxes; however, many organizations treat uppercase and lowercase letters as equivalent.</p>
<p>Despite the wide range of special characters which are technically valid; organisations, mail services, mail servers and mail clients in practice often do not accept all of them. For example, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_Live_Hotmail" class="mw-redirect" title="Windows Live Hotmail">Windows Live Hotmail</a> only allows creation of email addresses using alphanumerics, dot (<code>.</code>), underscore (<code>_</code>) and hyphen (<code>-</code>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Common advice is to avoid using some special characters to avoid the risk of rejected emails.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain">Domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> part of an email address has to conform to strict guidelines: it must match the requirements for a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hostname" title="Hostname">hostname</a>, a list of dot-separated <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNS" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS">DNS</a> labels, each label being limited to a length of 63 characters and consisting of:<sup id="cite_ref-rfc3696_7-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-rfc3696-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>uppercase and lowercase <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Latin_(Unicode_block)" title="Basic Latin (Unicode block)">Latin</a> letters <code>A</code> to <code>Z</code> and <code>a</code> to <code>z</code>;</li>
<li>digits <code>0</code> to <code>9</code>, provided that top-level domain names are not all-numeric;</li>
<li>hyphen <code>-</code>, provided that it is not the first or last character.</li>
</ul>
<p>This rule is known as the <i>LDH rule</i> (letters, digits, hyphen). In addition, the domain may be an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> literal, surrounded by square brackets <code>[]</code>, such as <code>jsmith@[192.168.2.1]</code> or <code>jsmith@[IPv6:2001:db8::1]</code>, although this is rarely seen except in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_spam" title="Email spam">email spam</a>. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">Internationalized domain names</a> (which are encoded to comply with the requirements for a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hostname" title="Hostname">hostname</a>) allow for presentation of non-ASCII domains. In mail systems compliant with <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6531">RFC 6531</a> and <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6532">RFC 6532</a> an email address may be encoded as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a>, both a local-part as well as a domain name.</p>
<p>Comments are allowed in the domain as well as in the local-part; for example, <code>john.smith@(comment)example.com</code> and <code>john.smith@example.com(comment)</code> are equivalent to <code>john.smith@example.com</code>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_local-part_semantics">Common local-part semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Common local-part semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5321">RFC 5321</a> 2.3.11 <i>Mailbox and Address,</i> "...the local-part MUST be interpreted and assigned semantics only by the host specified in the domain of the address." This means that no assumptions can be made about the meaning of the local-part of another mail server. It is entirely up to the configuration of the mail server.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local-part_normalization">Local-part normalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Local-part normalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interpretation of the <i>local part</i> of an email address is dependent on the conventions and policies implemented in the mail server. For example, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Case_sensitivity" title="Case sensitivity">case sensitivity</a> may distinguish mailboxes differing only in capitalization of characters of the local-part, although this is not very common.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> ignores all dots in the local-part for the purposes of determining account identity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This prevents the creation of user accounts <i>your.user.name</i> or <i>yourusername</i> when the account <i>your.username</i> already exists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-addressing">Sub-addressing<span id="Address_tags"></span><span id="Sub-addressing"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sub-addressing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some mail services support a tag appended to the local-part, such that the modified address is an alias to the unmodified one. For example, the address <i>joeuser+tag@example.com</i> denotes the same delivery address as <i>joeuser@example.com</i>. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5233">RFC 5233</a>, refers to this convention as <i>sub-addressing</i>, but it is also known as <i>plus addressing</i> or <i>tagged addressing</i>.</p>
<p>Addresses of this form, using various separators between the base name and the tag, are supported by several email services, including <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runbox" title="Runbox">Runbox</a> (plus), <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> (plus),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo!_Mail" title="Yahoo! Mail">Yahoo! Mail</a> Plus (hyphen),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Apple's <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICloud" title="ICloud">iCloud</a> (plus), <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outlook.com" title="Outlook.com">Outlook.com</a> (plus),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FastMail" title="FastMail">FastMail</a> (plus and Subdomain Addressing),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MMDF" title="MMDF">MMDF</a> (equals), <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qmail" title="Qmail">Qmail</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Courier_Mail_Server" title="Courier Mail Server">Courier Mail Server</a> (hyphen).<sup id="cite_ref-control_14-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-control-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postfix_(software)" title="Postfix (software)">Postfix</a> allows configuring an arbitrary separator from the legal character set.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The text of the tag may be used to apply filtering,<sup id="cite_ref-control_14-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-control-14">[14]</a></sup> or to create <i>single-use</i>, or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disposable_email_address" title="Disposable email address">disposable email addresses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In practice, the form validation of some web sites may reject special characters such as "+" in an email address – treating them, (incorrectly), as invalid characters. This can lead to an incorrect user receiving an e-mail if the "+" is silently stripped by a website without any warning or error messages. For example, an email intended for the user-entered email address foo+bar@example.com could be incorrectly sent to foobar@example.com. In other cases a poor user experience can occur if some parts of a site, such as a user registration page, allow the "+" character whilst other parts, such as a page for unsubscribing from a site's mailing list, do not.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Validation_and_verification">Validation and verification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Validation and verification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Email addresses are often requested as input to website as user identification for the purpose of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation" title="Data validation">data validation</a>. While there are companies that provide services to validate an email address at the time of entry, normally using an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">Application programming interface</a>, there is no guarantee that it will provide accurate results.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>An email address is generally recognized as having two parts joined with an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/At-sign" class="mw-redirect" title="At-sign">at-sign</a> (<i>@</i>). However, the technical specification detailed in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc822">RFC 822</a> and subsequent RFCs are more extensive.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expression</a> can be used to check for all of these criteria, except that of bracketed nested comments.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Syntactically correct, verified email addresses do not guarantee <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_box" title="Email box">email box</a> existence. Thus many mail servers use other techniques and check the mailbox existence against relevant systems such as the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> for the domain or using <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callback_verification" title="Callback verification">callback verification</a> to check if the mailbox exists. This is however often disabled to avoid <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directory_harvest_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Directory harvest attack">directory harvest attack</a>.</p>
<p>Assuring an email address is of a good quality requires a combination of various validation techniques. Large websites, bulk mailers and spammers require fast algorithms that predict validity of email address. Such methods depend heavily on <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heuristic_algorithm" class="mw-redirect" title="Heuristic algorithm">heuristic algorithms</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">statistical models</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many websites evaluate the validity of email addresses differently than the standards specify, rejecting addresses containing valid characters, such as <i>+</i> and <i>/</i>, or enforcing arbitrary length limitations. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3696">RFC 3696</a> provides specific advice for validating Internet identifiers, including email addresses.</p>
<p>HTML5 forms implemented in many browsers, allow email address validation to be handled by the browser.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Email address internationalization provides for a much larger range of characters than many current validation algorithms allow, such as all Unicode characters above U+0080, encoded as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_validation">Identity validation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Identity validation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Email addresses are the primary means of account activation (user identification and validation on websites), but other methods are available, such as cell phone number validation, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_mail" class="mw-redirect" title="Postal mail">postal mail</a> validation, fax validation. Email address validation is accomplished by the website sending an email with a special temporary hyperlink to the user-provided email address. On receipt, the user opens the link, immediately activating the account. Email addresses are also useful as means of forwarding messages from a website, e.g., user messages, user actions, to the email inbox.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internationalization">Internationalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Internationalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IETF" class="mw-redirect" title="IETF">IETF</a> conducts a technical and standards working group devoted to internationalization issues of email addresses, entitled <i>Email Address Internationalization</i> (EAI, also known as IMA, Internationalized Mail Address).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This group produced <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6530">RFC 6530</a>, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6531">RFC 6531</a>, <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6532">RFC 6532</a>, and <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6533">RFC 6533</a>, and continues to work on additional EAI-related RFCs.</p>
<p>The IETF's EAI Working group published <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6530">RFC 6530</a> "Overview and Framework for Internationalized Email", which enabled non-ASCII characters to be used in both the local-parts and domain of an email address. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6530">RFC 6530</a> provides for email based on the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a> encoding, which permits the full repertoire of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>. <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6531">RFC 6531</a> provides a mechanism for SMTP servers to negotiate transmission of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_SMTP#SMTPUTF8" title="Extended SMTP">SMTPUTF8</a> content.</p>
<p>The basic EAI concepts involve exchanging mail in UTF-8. Though the original proposal included a downgrading mechanism for legacy systems, this has now been dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The local servers are responsible for the local-part of the address, whereas the domain would be restricted by the rules of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">internationalized domain names</a>, though still transmitted in UTF-8. The mail server is also responsible for any mapping mechanism between the IMA form and any ASCII alias.</p>
<p>EAI enables users to have a localized address in a native language script or character set, as well as an ASCII form for communicating with legacy systems or for script-independent use. Applications that recognize internationalized domain names and mail addresses must have facilities to convert these representations.</p>
<p>Significant demand for such addresses is expected in China, Japan, Russia, and other markets that have large user bases in a non-Latin-based writing system. For example, in addition to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.in" title=".in">.in</a> top-level domain, the government of India in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> got approval for ".bharat", (from <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of India">Bhārat Gaṇarājya</a></i>), written in seven <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmic_scripts" title="Brahmic scripts">different scripts</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_address#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> for use by Gujrati, Marathi, Bangali, Tamil, Telugu, Punjabi and Urdu speakers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internationalization_examples">Internationalization examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Email_address&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Internationalization examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The example addresses below would not be handled by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5322">RFC 5322</a> based servers, but are permitted by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6530">RFC 6530</a>. Servers compliant with this will be able to handle these:</p>
<ul>
<li>Latin Alphabet (with diacritics): Pelé@example.com</li>
<li>Greek Alphabet: δοκιμή@παράδειγμα.δοκιμή</li>
<li>Traditional Chinese Characters: 我買@屋企.香港</li>
<li>Japanese Characters: 甲斐@黒川.日本</li>
<li>Cyrillic Characters: чебурашка@ящик-с-апельсинами.рф</li>
<li>Hindi Email Address: संपर्क@डाटामेल.भारत</li>
</ul>
